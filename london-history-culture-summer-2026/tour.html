1

St. Dunstan in the East

20 minutes

A secret garden hidden inside the shell of a bombed-out medieval church. This is one of the most atmospheric and peaceful spots in London, and most tourists walk right past it.

Look for the trees growing through the arched windows of the Wren-designed tower. The contrast between the grey stone ruins and the lush green vines is stunning.

Originally built around 1100, this church was severely damaged in the Great Fire of 1666, patched up by Sir Christopher Wren, and finally destroyed by the Blitz in 1941. Instead of rebuilding, the City turned it into a public garden in 1971.

Insider tip: It opens at 8 AM. If you start your tour early, you might have this enchanting space entirely to yourself for photos.

Tower of London

30 minutes (perimeter walk)

A UNESCO World Heritage site and the most secure castle in the land for nearly 1,000 years. It has been a palace, fortress, prison, and zoo.

Look for Traitors' Gate from the embankment side, where prisoners like Anne Boleyn entered by water. Watch for the ravens on the lawn inside the walls.

Built by William the Conqueror in the 1070s to oppress the rebellious Londoners. Legend says that if the six ravens ever leave the Tower, the Kingdom will fall. To be safe, there are usually seven (one spare), and their wings are clipped.

Insider tip: You don't need a ticket to see the best views. Walk the riverside path for a perfect view of the White Tower and the medieval walls without the crowds inside.

Tower Bridge

20 minutes (crossing)

The world's most famous bascule bridge. It defines the London skyline and is a Victorian engineering marvel, opened in 1894.

Look for the bascules (see-saw pivots) at the base of the towers. Look through the gaps in the pedestrian railing to see the river churning below.

In 1952, a bus driver had to jump his double-decker bus across a 3-foot gap when the bridge started opening while he was still on it. He made it.

Insider tip: Check the Bridge Lift Times online before you go. Seeing the bridge open for a large ship is a lucky bonus.

Shad Thames / Butler's Wharf

15 minutes

This historic cobblestone street looks exactly like 19th-century industrial London. It was the Larder of London where tea, coffee, and spices were stored.

Look for the wrought-iron overhead gantries (bridges) connecting the warehouses. These were used to roll barrels of goods between buildings without going down to the muddy street.

In the Victorian era, this area smelled of spices from the East Indies. This street is a favorite film location - you might recognize it from Bridget Jones's Diary or Cruella.

Insider tip: The acoustics here are incredible; your footsteps will echo off the brick canyons.

Borough Market

60 minutes (lunch stop)

London's oldest and most renowned food market, dating back to the 12th century. It is a sensory explosion of smells, sights, and tastes.

Look for the Market Bell and the Art Deco green glass structure. It sits right next to Southwark Cathedral - monks used to trade grain here in the Middle Ages.

Historically, this market was outside the City jurisdiction, making it a place for chaotic trade and lawlessness.

Insider tip: Don't commit to the first thing you see. Do a "tasting lap" first. Most vendors offer free samples of cheese and charcuterie.

Where to Eat at Borough Market

The George Inn

30 minutes (drink stop)

The last remaining galleried coaching inn in London. It is owned by the National Trust and looks like stepping back into the 1600s.

Look for the wooden galleries (balconies) overlooking the courtyard. This was the bus terminal of the 1600s for coaches heading south.

Charles Dickens was a regular here. He mentions it in Little Dorrit. His life insurance policy was famously found behind the bar after he died, though this might be a tall tale.

Insider tip: Even if you don't drink, step into the Parliament Bar to see the ancient fireplaces and uneven floors.

7

Shakespeare's Globe

15 minutes

A faithful reconstruction of the open-air playhouse where Shakespeare worked. Look for the thatched roof (the only one allowed in Central London since the Great Fire) and the oak timber frame.

The original stood a few hundred feet away until it burned down in 1613 during a performance of Henry VIII when a cannon misfired.

American actor Sam Wanamaker spent decades fighting to get this built when he was shocked to find only a plaque marking the original site in the 1950s.

Insider tip: You can see the groundlings standing in the yard through the iron gates if a rehearsal or tour is happening.

Tate Modern

30 minutes

The world's most popular modern art gallery, housed in the former Bankside Power Station. Converted by Herzog & de Meuron in 2000, it revitalized the entire South Bank.

Look for the Turbine Hall. It is a cavernous, cathedral-like industrial space that hosts massive, changing art installations.

Insider tip: Head to the viewing terrace on Level 10 of the Blavatnik Building (the new extension) for a panoramic 360-degree view of London. It's often less crowded than the Shard and usually free.

Millennium Bridge

15 minutes (crossing)

The Wobbly Bridge (it doesn't wobble anymore). A pedestrian-only Blade of Light connecting the spiritual (St Paul's) with the cultural (Tate).

Look for the tiny chewing gum art on the bridge deck. Artist Ben Wilson paints miniature scenes on discarded gum trodden into the bridge.

Opened in 2000, it closed 2 days later due to swaying. Reopened 2 years later after engineering fixes.

Insider tip: This is the best photo op for St. Paul's Cathedral. Frame the dome perfectly between the bridge supports.

St. Paul's Cathedral

End of tour

Sir Christopher Wren's masterpiece and the spiritual heart of the nation. Rebuilt after the Great Fire of 1666, it survived the Blitz while the area around it burned.

Look for the pineapples on the top of the western towers - symbols of hospitality.

This is where Prince Charles married Lady Diana, and where Winston Churchill had his funeral.

Insider tip: If you still have energy, the One New Change shopping center next door has a public roof terrace (free) with a stunning eye-level view of the Dome.
