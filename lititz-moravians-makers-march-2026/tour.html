Moravians, Makers & Cocoa Beans

Lititz, Pennsylvania · Late March

America's coolest small town blends deep Moravian religious history with an industrial heritage of chocolate and pretzels. Walk the same streets as 18th-century settlers and taste traditions that predate Hershey.

Before You Go

Best start time: 10:00 AM (beat pretzel crowds, finish for lunch)
Parking: Lititz Springs Park lot (free) on North Broad Street
Weather: March: 35-55 degrees Fahrenheit, bring layers and waterproof shoes
Budget: About 30 to 50 dollars (tours, chocolate, food)

1

Lititz Springs Park & Welcome Center

20 min 24 North Broad Street, Lititz

This park is the beating heart of Lititz, and it has been for centuries. Long before the Moravians arrived, Native Americans gathered at this natural spring. When the settlers came in 1756, this reliable water source made their community possible.

Wander toward the spring itself and look for the Lion's Head carved into the stone wall near the water. A local artist created this in the 1800s, and it's become a beloved landmark. The Victorian-era train station nearby now serves as the Welcome Center, staffed by volunteers who know every corner of this town.

Dedicated in 1856, this park hosts the oldest continuous Fourth of July celebration in the United States. During the Victorian era, trains would offload thousands of city dwellers here for picnics. The tradition of community gathering has never stopped.

Insider Tip

Stop into the Welcome Center to grab a physical map. The volunteers are incredibly knowledgeable and can share stories you won't find in any guidebook.

2

Wilbur Chocolate Store

20 min 45 North Broad Street, Lititz

Here's a piece of chocolate trivia that will change how you see Pennsylvania: Wilbur Buds were created in 1894, a full thirteen years before the Hershey Kiss appeared in 1907. This is the original, the one that started it all.

The original factory is now condos, but this store keeps the legacy alive with pride. Inside, look for the candy kitchen viewing area where you can watch chocolatiers hand-dipping treats using techniques that haven't changed in over a century.

H.O. Wilbur and Sons moved here in the early 1900s. For decades, the aroma of chocolate production would drift over the entire town. Locals will tell you they could predict the weather by how strong the chocolate smell was in the air.

Insider Tip

Don't just buy the Wilbur Buds. Try the chocolate-covered pretzels for a localized sweet and salty combination that captures both of Lititz's famous industries in one bite.

3

Julius Sturgis Pretzel Bakery

30-45 min 219 East Main Street, Lititz

You're standing at the first commercial pretzel bakery in America, operating in a house that was built in 1784. Julius Sturgis started baking here in 1861, and the building itself is one of the oldest standing structures in town.

Inside, look for the original brick ovens and the indented stone on the window sill where apprentices supposedly rested their elbows during their long shifts. The building has been preserved with remarkable care, and you can almost smell the history alongside the fresh dough.

Legend has it that a hobo gave a baker the recipe for a hard pretzel in exchange for a meal. Whether or not that's true, it's a story that launched an entire industry. Pennsylvania Dutch pretzels became famous across America, and it all started right here.

Insider Tip

You can just visit the shop, but the tour at about four to five dollars lets you try twisting your own soft pretzel. Trust me: it's much harder than it looks, and you'll have new respect for the craft.

4

Linden Hall School for Girls

10 min 212 East Main Street, Lititz (exterior view)

Founded in 1746, Linden Hall is the oldest girls' boarding school in the United States. Let that date sink in: this institution was educating young women thirty years before the Declaration of Independence was signed.

The Moravians believed strongly in equal education for women, a radical concept in the 18th century that set them apart from their contemporaries. Look for the Mary Dixon Memorial Chapel and observe the pristine, uniform colonial architecture connecting the buildings.

During the Revolutionary War, like many buildings in town, this school was pressed into service as a military hospital. The walls here have witnessed the birth of a nation.

Insider Tip

If the gates are open, walk through the campus courtyard. It feels like stepping directly back into the 1700s, with beautifully maintained grounds and architecture untouched by time.

5

Lititz Moravian Church Square

20 min 8 Church Square, Lititz

This is the spiritual and architectural anchor of the entire town. The square includes the Church built in 1787, the Brothers' House, and the Sisters' House, all arranged according to Moravian community planning principles.

Look carefully for the small stone building to the side called the Corpse House, or Leichenkapellchen in German. Bodies were kept here for three days before burial, a Moravian tradition. Behind the church, the cemetery known as God's Acre contains a remarkable sight: all gravestones are flat and equal in size, symbolizing that all are equal in death.

For a long time, only Moravians were allowed to live in Lititz. This closed community didn't open to outsiders until 1855. You're walking through a place that maintained its religious isolation for nearly a century.

Insider Tip

Look for the separate entrances for men and women on the older buildings. This reflects the Choir system, where the community was divided by age, gender, and marital status—a social organization unique to the Moravians.

Photo Op

The view from the center of the square looking toward the church captures the peaceful colonial atmosphere perfectly. Early morning light is best.

6

Johannes Mueller House

30 min 137 East Main Street, Lititz

This 1792 house offers something rare: an authentic glimpse into the life of an 18th-century working tradesman rather than a wealthy elite family. Johannes Mueller was a dyer, and his home reflects the practical concerns of a craftsman.

Look for the distinct Moravian architectural details, particularly the herringbone-patterned doors that were a signature of local craftsmen. The house retains its original character, and costumed guides explain the daily rhythms of early settler life.

Ask about the hidden chamber and the strict rules of the town council that Mueller had to follow just to live here. The Moravian community regulated everything from building placement to acceptable trades.

Insider Tip

In March, they may operate on spring hours, usually weekends only. If the house is closed, the garden behind it is still a beautiful and quiet spot to sit and reflect before heading to lunch.

F

Bulls Head Public House

45-60 min 14 East Main Street (inside General Sutter Inn)

You've earned a proper meal, and this is the place to have it. The Bulls Head is an authentic British-style pub that's been recognized globally—literally voted one of the best beer bars in the world in online polls.

The pub sits inside the General Sutter Inn, which dates back to 1764. The building itself is a piece of history, named after John Sutter of California Gold Rush fame who spent his final years in Lititz.

The atmosphere is lively but not overwhelming, with dark wood, a proper bar, and a selection of craft and cask ales that would impress anyone. Perfect for an adventurous couple who appreciates both history and quality.

What to Order

The Fish and Chips are excellent, as are the Scotch Eggs and the Shepherd's Pie. Expect to spend fifteen to twenty-five dollars per person. Be prepared for a lively atmosphere around one o'clock.

Alternative Option

For something lighter, try the Tomato Pie Cafe at 23 North Broad Street near the park. Their savory tomato tart and excellent espresso offer a more casual option at about twelve to twenty dollars per person.

Until Next Time

You've walked through a town that's been called America's coolest small town, and now you understand why. From Moravian settlers who believed in equality before it was fashionable, to the entrepreneurs who created iconic American foods, Lititz has always punched above its weight. The chocolate and pretzels are delicious, but the real treat is the living history you've just experienced. Safe travels home.
