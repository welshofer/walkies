Royal Munich and The River Wave

Munich, Germany

From Baroque excess to royal treasures, bustling markets to urban surfers. Bavarian opulence meets modern Munich in one unforgettable walk.

Before You Go

Best start time: 9:30 AM to catch the 11 AM Glockenspiel
Start point: Sendlinger Tor U-Bahn (U1, U2, U3, U6)
Weather: 8 to 20 degrees C, layers essential
Budget: Tower 5 EUR, Treasury 9 EUR, Lunch 30+ EUR

1

Asamkirche

15 minutes Sendlinger Strasse 32

A masterpiece of late Baroque architecture packed into a tiny space, only 8 by 27 meters. Built in the 1700s by the Asam brothers as their private chapel, they bypassed strict church building codes and went wild with Rococo decoration.

Look for the Asam window above the altar. This hidden window lights up the crucifix from behind, creating an almost theatrical effect. The ceiling fresco creates an optical illusion of endless height.

The brothers lived next door. You can spot a window from the priest's house looking directly onto the high altar, which allowed Egid Asam to see the altar from his bed.

Insider Tip

The church opens at 9 AM. It is best viewed when quiet to appreciate the overwhelming detail without crowds.

700 meters 10 minutes

2

St. Peter's Church (Alter Peter)

30 minutes Rindermarkt 1

The oldest parish church in Munich, founded in the 11th century before the city was even officially established. For an active couple, the tower climb offers the absolute best panorama of the city and the Alps on a clear spring day.

Inside, look for the glass coffin of Saint Munditia, patron saint of single women. It is a gem-encrusted skeleton that is equal parts fascinating and macabre.

Locals say the clocks on this tower run slightly faster than the ones on the City Hall because Peter is older and has seen it all before.

Insider Tip

If the weather is clear, especially with the Foehn wind, you can see all the way to the Zugspitze. Check visibility at the ticket booth before climbing the 306 steps.

Photo Op

The tower offers the definitive Munich skyline shot: the twin onion domes of the Frauenkirche, the red roofs of the old town, and the Alps beyond.

150 meters 2 minutes

Walk behind St. Peter's church directly into the open square of Viktualienmarkt. It is an immediate transition from spiritual quiet to bustling market life.

3

Viktualienmarkt

30 minutes Viktualienmarkt 3

Munich's culinary heart. This is an open-air market with over 140 stalls selling gourmet cheese, game, flowers, and exotic fruit. Originally a farmers' market on Marienplatz, it was moved here in 1807 by King Maximilian I.

Look for the Maypole in the center with figures representing local guilds. The market fountains feature statues of famous Munich folk singers and comedians like Karl Valentin.

Insider Tip

Do not just look; sample. Buy a pickle from a barrel or fresh horseradish. The beer garden here rotates which brewery it serves, a unique trait in Munich.

Food Stop Option

Fisch Witte at Viktualienmarkt 9 is a fishmonger that turns into a chic bistro. Order the grilled fish platter or oysters with a glass of Riesling.

Walk to Stop 4
300 meters 4 minutes

Walk back from the market towards the massive church towers, turning left towards the open Marienplatz square.

4

Marienplatz and Glockenspiel

15 minutes Marienplatz 1

The city's main square since 1158. The New Town Hall is a Gothic Revival spectacle. Named after the Column of Mary, erected in 1638 to celebrate the end of Swedish occupation during the Thirty Years' War.

Look for the Dragon on the facade of the Town Hall, climbing the turrets. The Glockenspiel plays at 11 AM and 12 PM. The top level reenacts a royal wedding tournament. Bavaria wins, naturally. The bottom level performs the Coopers' Dance, performed to cheer citizens after the plague.

Insider Tip

Instead of craning your neck in the square, go to the top floor of the Hugendubel bookstore or Cafe Glockenspiel for a window-seat view of the mechanical show.

Walk to Stop 5
650 meters 8 minutes

From Marienplatz, walk past Dallmayr Delicatessen. Worth a peek inside; it is the Harrods of Munich. Continue north on Residenzstrasse. Rub the lion's nose on the shields to the left. Locals believe it brings luck and wealth.

5

Munich Residenz Treasury

45 minutes Residenzstrasse 1

The former royal palace of the Wittelsbach monarchs. It is the largest city palace in Germany. Served as the seat of government and residence of Bavarian dukes, electors, and kings from 1508 to 1918.

Focus your visit on the Treasury to stay on schedule. It houses the crown jewels, including the prayer book of Emperor Charles the Bald from 860 AD and the statue of St. George made of 2,291 diamonds.

Insider Tip

The ticket for just the Treasury is cheaper and quicker than the full museum, perfect for a high-impact, shorter visit.

Lunch Option

Spatenhaus an der Oper at Residenzstrasse 12 is refined and traditional. Order the Wiener Schnitzel with cranberry sauce or the Bavarian Duck. The upstairs dining room offers views of the Opera House.

Walk to Stop 6
900 meters 12 minutes

Exit the Residenz and walk through the Hofgarten, the Court Garden. Notice the Temple of Diana in the center, often a spot for tango dancers. Exit at the northeast corner, cross the street to the English Garden. You will hear the rushing water before you see the surfers.

6

Eisbachwelle

15 minutes Prinzregentenstrasse, near Haus der Kunst

A continuous, man-made wave on the Eisbach river where surfers ride year-round, even in snow. It captures Munich's unique blend of high culture and outdoor obsession. Surfing here started illegally in the 70s and was only officially legalized in 2010.

Look for the queue of surfers on the bank. There is a strict hierarchy and etiquette for who drops in next. Jack Johnson and Kelly Slater have both surfed here.

Insider Tip

Cross the small bridge to watch from the other side for a better angle of their footwork. Late afternoon golden hour light makes for spectacular photographs.

Photo Op

The contrast of a wetsuit-clad surfer against the classical architecture of the Haus der Kunst museum is quintessential modern Munich.

F

Schneider Braeuhaus

End of tour option Tal 7, near Marienplatz

More authentic and food-focused than the Hofbraeuhaus. Locals actually eat here. This is where you experience real Bavarian beer hall culture.

What to Order

The Aventinus, a wheat doppelbock with complex fruit and spice notes, and the Kronfleisch, skirt steak that is a kitchen classic. In Bavaria, you can bring your own food to beer gardens, but only in the self-service areas.

The End of Your Walk

You have walked through centuries of Bavarian history, from private Baroque chapels to royal treasure rooms, from medieval market squares to the thoroughly modern spectacle of urban surfing. Munich rewards those who look beyond the beer hall cliches. Watch for the Fruehlingsfest at Theresienwiese in late April, the Little Oktoberfest, if you want to extend your Munich adventure.
