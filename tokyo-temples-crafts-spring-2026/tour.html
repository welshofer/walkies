The Nostalgic Heart of Tokyo

Tokyo, Japan · Spring

Temples, cherry blossoms, craft workshops, and street food in Yanaka, a rare pocket of old Tokyo that survived war and earthquakes.

Before You Go

Best start time: 1:30 PM for golden hour at Sunset Stairs
Start point: JR Nippori Station, North Exit
Transit: JR Yamanote Line to Nippori
Budget: 3,000 to 5,000 yen (food and drinks)

1

Tennoji Temple

15 minutes 7-14-8 Yanaka, Taito-ku

Founded in 1274, this is the oldest temple in Yanaka and sets the spiritual tone for your walk. Unlike the tourist-packed temples elsewhere in Tokyo, Tennoji sits peacefully away from the crowds, its moss-covered grounds humming with ancient energy.

Look for the Tennoji Daibutsu, a large bronze Buddha statue cast in 1690 that sits serenely on the left as you enter. Notice how the modern, clean-lined architecture of the main hall contrasts with the ancient grounds around it.

This area was historically a "temple town" called Teramachi, created by the Shogunate to protect Edo Castle from evil spirits thought to come from the northeast.

Insider Tip

Look for the rare mochi-pounding rabbit statue near the entrance, which is said to bring good luck. This temple was once a gambling den! The shogunate allowed a lottery here, drawing massive crowds until it was banned for corrupting public morals.

Photo Op

The bronze Buddha with the temple's clean modern architecture rising behind it creates a striking composition of old and new.

Walk to Stop 2
300 meters 5 minutes

Exit the temple and turn left. You are now entering the main path of Yanaka Cemetery. Walk straight down the tree-lined central avenue.

2

Yanaka Cemetery and Sakura-dori

30 minutes 7-5-24 Yanaka, Taito-ku

Far from spooky, this is one of Tokyo's most beautiful public parks and a premier cherry blossom viewing spot. In late March and early April, the central Sakura-dori becomes a stunning tunnel of pink blossoms, petals drifting down like snow.

Opened in 1874, it was one of Japan's first public cemeteries. The majestic ancient cherry trees form a natural canopy over the road, creating one of Tokyo's most photographed springtime scenes.

Look for the fenced-off grave of Tokugawa Yoshinobu, the very last Shogun of Japan. He is buried here in a Shinto-style grave, unusual for a Shogun, because he retired peacefully to save Edo from war during the Meiji Restoration.

Insider Tip

Keep an eye out for the friendly cemetery cats that lounge on tombstones. They are well cared for by locals and have become local celebrities, even spawning fan merchandise.

Walk to Stop 3
450 meters 7 minutes

Continue south through the cemetery until you exit the main gate. Turn left, then take the first right into the residential Ueno Sakuragi area. Look for a cluster of three preserved wooden houses.

3

Ueno Sakuragi Atari

20 minutes 2-15-6 Ueno Sakuragi, Taito-ku

A brilliant example of adaptive reuse. These three houses built in 1938 have been renovated into a community complex with a bakery, beer hall, and olive oil shop, preserving the Showa-era aesthetic while giving the buildings new life.

These were private residences that miraculously survived the war. They were saved from demolition by a local preservation NPO who transformed them into this charming compound. It is a rare chance to step inside a traditional pre-war Japanese home without taking your shoes off, at least in the public areas.

Mid-Tour Break

The Yanaka Beer Hall inside one of the houses serves local craft brews. Order a small glass of Yanaka Beer, a local craft brew in either Dry or Golden style, and sip it on the terrace if weather permits. The smoked nuts pair perfectly.

Walk to Stop 4
150 meters 3 minutes

Exit the complex and turn right. Walk to the main intersection called Kotomoto and you will see a building with a tiled roof and a modern glass entrance.

4

SCAI The Bathhouse

15 minutes 6-1-23 Yanaka, Taito-ku

One of Tokyo's most prestigious contemporary art galleries, housed in a 200-year-old former public bathhouse called sento. The contrast between the avant-garde art inside and the traditional tiled roof outside is striking and uniquely Japanese.

Look for the original wooden shoe lockers at the entrance and notice the unusually high ceiling, which is where the steam vents used to be. Kashiwayu, the original bathhouse, was a beloved community hub until it closed in 1991. It reopened as a gallery in 1993.

Insider Tip

Admission is free. It is a quick but visually powerful stop that perfectly captures Yanaka's ability to preserve the old while embracing the new.

Walk to Stop 5
10 meters 1 minute

It is diagonally across the street from SCAI. Look for the distinctive wooden exterior.

5

Kayaba Coffee

10 minutes 6-1-29 Yanaka, Taito-ku

An iconic symbol of Yanaka. This 1916 building was originally a milk hall, then became a coffee shop, and is beloved for its retro Taisho-Roman architecture. The distinct wooden exterior and steep staircase visible through the window make it one of the most photographed buildings in the neighborhood.

Insider Tip

If the line is long, and it usually is, just admire the architecture from outside. If you do get a table, their Egg Sandwich is legendary: a fluffy omelet inside soft white bread that melts in your mouth.

Walk to Stop 6
900 meters 15 minutes

From Kayaba Coffee, head west down the winding residential streets. Aim for Hebi-michi, which means Snake Road, a winding street built over a former stream. It zigs and zags between houses. Follow it all the way to Nezu Shrine.

Yanaka is a quiet residential area. Please keep voices down in the small backstreets, especially along Hebi-michi.

6

Nezu Shrine

40 minutes 1-28-9 Nezu, Bunkyo-ku

One of Japan's most beautiful shrines. The current buildings date from 1706 and are designated Important Cultural Properties. Legend says it was founded 1,900 years ago by Prince Yamato Takeru.

Walk through the mini Kyoto tunnel of vermilion torii gates. In late March and early April, the Azalea Garden called Tsutsuji-en will be starting to bloom in spectacular pinks and purples. The 3,000 bushes create a carpet of color on the hillside.

The shrine was a favorite of literary giants like Natsume Soseki and Mori Ogai, who lived nearby and drew inspiration from its tranquil grounds.

Insider Tip

Find the Otome Inari shrine on the hill behind the torii gates for a fantastic elevated view of the temple grounds looking down through the trees. The Bunkyo Azalea Festival usually begins in early April, so visiting in late March means you beat the crowds but still catch the early blooms.

Photo Op

The tunnel of vermilion torii gates creates the iconic shot, but try also capturing the azaleas with the shrine buildings in the background for a uniquely Nezu composition.

7

Yanaka Ginza Shopping Street

45 minutes 3-13-1 Yanaka, Taito-ku

The quintessential shitamachi shopping street. It is narrow, crowded, and lined with family-run shops selling crafts, tea, and street food. This is where locals actually shop, not a tourist reconstruction.

Look for the Seven Lucky Cats, statues hidden on rooftops and signs along the street. Finding all seven is a local tradition that brings good luck.

Insider Tip

At the far end you will find Yuyake Dandan, the Sunset Stairs. If you timed your walk right by starting at 1:30 PM, you will arrive here around golden hour to watch the sunset over the district. The view from the top of the stairs, looking down over the rooftops bathed in amber light, is unforgettable.

F

Niku no Sato and Hantei

Street food or sit-down meal Yanaka Ginza and 2-12-15 Nezu

You have two excellent options depending on your mood and hunger level.

For a quick bite in Yanaka Ginza, stop at Niku no Sato, a butcher shop serving legendary menchi katsu, deep-fried minced meat cutlets. Eating a hot, crispy menchi katsu while walking down the shopping street is the true Yanaka style. Only about 200 yen.

For a more substantial meal, Hantei near Nezu Shrine is a historic wooden building designated as a Tangible Cultural Property. They serve kushiage, deep-fried skewers, with a lunch set around 3,000 yen. The atmospheric setting in a pre-war building is perfect for a couple looking for something special.

What to Order

At Niku no Sato: Menchi katsu, eaten hot on the spot. At Hantei: The lunch set featuring an assortment of seasonal kushiage, each skewer a small work of art.

The End of Your Walk

You have wandered through one of Tokyo's last neighborhoods where the past remains tangible, from a 750-year-old temple to cherry blossoms, from craft beer in a 1938 house to street food on a shopping street that time forgot. Nippori Station is a short walk from the Sunset Stairs to catch the Yamanote Line anywhere in Tokyo.
