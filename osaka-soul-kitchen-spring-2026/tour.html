1

Shitennoji Temple

30 minutes 1-11-18 Shitennoji, Tennoji Ward

Founded in 593 AD by Prince Shotoku, this is officially the oldest Buddhist temple in Japan. It was the first built by the state and set the template for Japanese Buddhist architecture.

Look for the perfect symmetry of the layout, called Shitennoji Style, with the pagoda, main hall, and lecture hall arranged in a straight line. Though burned down and rebuilt many times, most recently after World War Two, the concrete reconstruction faithfully retains the 6th-century design.

Prince Shotoku built this temple to honor the Four Heavenly Kings, called Shitenno, who he believed helped him win a war against anti-Buddhist clans.

Insider Tip

Check the torii gate at the entrance. It is made of stone, which is rare for Japan, and dates back to 1294. It has survived many fires that destroyed all the wooden buildings around it.

2

Gokuraku-jodo Garden

20 minutes Inside Shitennoji Temple grounds, northeast corner

A hidden oasis representing the Buddhist paradise, known as the Pure Land Garden. In late March and April, the cherry blossoms here are spectacular and often less crowded than the major parks.

Look for the two ponds representing the "Ocean of Amida Buddha." The garden design is based on descriptions of the Western Paradise in Buddhist sutras. It uses borrowed scenery techniques, and though the modern city has risen around it, the design still pulls your focus inward to the water and stones.

Insider Tip

Look for the waterfall. The sound design is intentional, meant to drown out city noise and create a meditative atmosphere. Entry fee is 300 yen.

3

Isshin-ji Temple

15 minutes 2-8-69 Osaka, Tennoji Ward

One of the most unique and slightly eerie temples in Japan, famous for its Okotsu Butsu statues. The modern steel-and-glass main gate was designed by the high priest, who was also an architect, creating a striking contrast between old and new.

Since 1887, the temple has mixed the ashes of hundreds of thousands of deceased devotees with resin to create Buddha statues. The original statues were destroyed in World War Two, but the practice resumed. The current statues contain the remains of over 2 million people.

Insider Tip

This is a very active place of worship. You will see locals lighting candles and incense constantly. Observe respectfully and quietly.

4

Keitakuen Garden

25 minutes 1-10-135 Chausuyamacho, Tennoji Ward

A magnificent stroll garden with a central pond, gifted by the Sumitomo merchant family to the city of Osaka. Designed by Ogawa Jihei, a legend of modern Japanese garden design, in 1926.

Look for the central pond with its stepping stones and the surrounding cherry trees reflecting in the water. This land was originally the private residence of the Sumitomo zaibatsu, one of Japan's great business conglomerates. They donated it so the public could enjoy nature in the heart of the city.

Insider Tip

The garden is designed to look larger than it is. The "mountains" in the background are actually man-made hills, cleverly positioned to block the view of nearby trains. Closed Mondays. Entry fee is 150 yen.

Photo Op

The reflections of cherry trees in the pond water create stunning compositions, especially on calm mornings.

Walk to Stop 5
600 meters 10 minutes

Tsutenkaku Tower and Shinsekai

20 minutes 1-18-6 Ebisuhigashi, Naniwa Ward

The symbol of Osaka. Shinsekai, meaning "New World," was built in 1912 as a futuristic entertainment district combining Paris in the northern half and New York in the southern half. Look for the flashy, oversized 3D signage of pufferfish, billiard balls, and kushikatsu chefs.

The original tower from 1912 looked like the Eiffel Tower sitting on top of the Arc de Triomphe. It was scrapped for metal during World War Two and rebuilt in 1956 in its current distinctive form.

Insider Tip

Do not go up the tower if the line is long, which is often 60 minutes or more. The view from below, amidst the neon chaos, is the real attraction. Look at the tower's top at night: the neon lights forecast tomorrow's weather. White means clear, orange means cloudy, blue means rain.

Walk to Stop 6
150 meters 2 minutes

Walk south from the tower into the covered shopping arcade.

6

Janjan Yokocho

15 minutes 3 Chome Ebisuhigashi, Naniwa Ward

A narrow, energetic alleyway packed with standing bars, shogi (Japanese chess) parlors, and pure retro vibes. Look for the Billiken statues, small figures representing the "god of things as they ought to be," and watch locals playing Go or Shogi in glass-walled clubs.

The name "Janjan" comes from the sound of shamisen, traditional Japanese banjos, that used to be played here to attract customers in the post-war era. This area was once a gritty laborer's district and retains a "rough but warm" Osaka character distinct from the polished north side of the city.

Insider Tip

This is the best place to feel the Showa era atmosphere, the period from 1926 to 1989. The vibe here has barely changed in decades.

7

Smartball Kasuga

15 minutes 3-6-10 Ebisuhigashi, Naniwa Ward

A rare survivor! Play Smartball, a vintage pinball-like game from the 1950s that has vanished from most of Japan. Look for the mechanical glass tables and listen to the satisfying sound of balls clacking. Before digital arcades, this was the entertainment.

For 100 yen, you get 25 balls. If you win, you get snacks or candy, not money. It is pure, low-stakes fun and a window into how Osaka entertained itself decades ago.

Insider Tip

It is a game of skill, not just luck. Watch the older locals to see how they control the plunger force. They have been playing for decades.

F

Kushikatsu in Shinsekai

Lunch or early dinner Shinsekai district

Shinsekai is the birthplace of kushikatsu, deep-fried skewers that are Osaka's most iconic street food. You have several excellent options depending on your style.

Kushikatsu Yakko is a counter-only spot that is quieter and more respectful than the tourist chains, perfect for a couple wanting quality over gimmicks. Order the beef skewer, asparagus, and mochi.

For atmosphere, try Ohsho Club, housed in a former movie theater from the Taisho era. It has a jazzy, nostalgic vibe and offers more refined ingredients. Try the Emperor Shrimp, a huge prawn, or seasonal vegetable sets.

Grill Bon, established in 1961, serves yoshoku, Japanese-Western style cuisine. It is famous for its beef cutlet sandwich, called hekatsu-sando.

Important Rule

Never double-dip your skewer in the communal sauce! Use the provided cabbage to scoop sauce onto your food instead. Breaking this rule will earn you stern looks from locals.

The End of Your Walk

You have traveled 1,400 years in just 2.5 kilometers, from Prince Shotoku's vision of Buddhist Japan to the neon-lit future that 1912 Osaka imagined. The warmth, humor, and unapologetic character of this neighborhood is the essence of Osaka itself. Catch the Midosuji Line from Dobutsuen-mae Station or the JR Line from Shin-Imamiya to continue your adventures.
