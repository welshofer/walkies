Origins to Elegance

Paris, France · A Tale of Two Banks

From the medieval heart of the Seine islands through the intellectual Left Bank to the grandeur of royal palaces. Walk where revolutions began, where writers found their muse, and where France itself was born.

1

Île Saint-Louis

20 minutes Quai de Bourbon, 75004 Paris

Unlike the rest of Paris, this island has remained largely unchanged since the 17th century. It is an architectural time capsule of French nobility. Where the rest of Paris sprawled and modernized, Île Saint-Louis preserved its original character through sheer stubbornness and good fortune.

This island was originally a cow pasture until the 1600s when it was developed as a unified real estate project for the wealthy. The result is a rare coherence in Parisian architecture: every building speaks the same elegant dialect of Louis XIII style.

Look for the mascarons, the ornate stone faces carved into Pont Marie. These grotesque decorations were believed to ward off evil spirits. Notice the elegant hôtels particuliers, private mansions, along the quay. Each doorway tells a story of old money and older taste.

Insider Tip

Walk down the stone stairs to the riverbank, the berges, for a quiet waterline view of the city away from traffic. In summer, locals picnic here with wine and cheese. This is the real Paris.

Sweet Break

Berthillon at 31 Rue Saint-Louis en l'Île serves what many consider the best ice cream in Paris. Try the fraise des bois, wild strawberry, or caramel au beurre salé, salted caramel. Cash only.

Notre-Dame de Paris

30 minutes 6 Parvis Notre-Dame, 75004 Paris

The literal and symbolic center of France. Point Zéro, the bronze star embedded in the cobblestones of the plaza, marks the spot from which all distances in France are measured. Step on it. Legend says you will return to Paris.

Built over nearly 200 years from 1163 to 1345, Notre-Dame survived revolutions and wars before the devastating 2019 fire. Following its reopening in late 2024, seeing the restored spire and cleaned limestone is a moving experience. The cathedral glows now in ways it has not for centuries.

Victor Hugo wrote The Hunchback of Notre-Dame specifically to shame the city into restoring the then-decaying cathedral. His novel worked. The 19th-century restoration saved the building, and the 21st-century restoration has renewed it once more.

Photo Op

The best photo angle is not from the front. Walk to Square Jean XXIII behind the cathedral to see the flying buttresses in their full glory. Morning light is particularly beautiful here.

History Note

Napoleon crowned himself Emperor here in 1804, snatching the crown from the Pope's hands. The cathedral has witnessed every major moment in French history.

3

Shakespeare and Company

20 minutes 37 Rue de la Bûcherie, 75005 Paris

The most famous English-language bookstore in the world. This cramped, crooked building was a haven for the Lost Generation writers: Hemingway, Fitzgerald, Stein, Joyce. Their ghosts still haunt the narrow aisles and creaking floors.

Look for the wishing well in the floor and the tumbleweeds, the sleeping cots tucked among books where aspiring writers still sleep for free in exchange for working in the shop and writing. Founder George Whitman called it a socialist utopia masquerading as a bookstore.

The original shop was closed by Nazis in 1941 when the owner refused to sell her last copy of Finnegans Wake to a German officer. This second iteration opened in 1951 and was renamed to honor the original in 1964.

Insider Tip

Do not queue to get inside if the line is long. The café next door offers the same atmospheric experience with a direct view of Notre-Dame and excellent coffee. The books can wait.

4

Jardin du Luxembourg

40 minutes Rue de Médicis, 75006 Paris

The quintessential Parisian park. This is where locals go to read, play chess, and sail toy boats on the octagonal pond. Created in 1612 by Marie de Medici, widow of Henry IV, who wanted to recreate the Pitti Palace gardens of her native Florence.

The Medici Fountain is a romantic, shaded grotto that is one of the coolest spots in Paris on a hot day. Find a green metal chair, the iconic Luxembourg seats, and claim your spot. This is where Parisians come to escape the summer heat.

The French Senate meets in the Luxembourg Palace at the north end. The garden is technically their backyard, which they graciously share with the public. Notice how the formal French garden style gives way to English garden informality in the southern section.

Hidden Gem

Look for the miniature Statue of Liberty on the west side of the park. It is a hidden sibling to the one in New York, one of several models Bartholdi created before the final version.

Photo Op

The Medici Fountain frames perfectly for photos. Early morning offers the best light and fewest crowds. The goldfish in the fountain add unexpected color.

5

Saint-Germain-des-Prés

30 minutes 6 Place Saint-Germain des Prés, 75006 Paris

The birthplace of Existentialism. Sartre, de Beauvoir, and Camus wrote their masterpieces at the café tables of Les Deux Magots and Café de Flore. The intellectual heart of 20th-century Paris still beats here, though now it shares space with luxury boutiques.

The bell tower of the church is the oldest in Paris, dating back to 990 AD. The name means of the fields because this was once a powerful abbey outside the city walls, surrounded by meadows. The Revolution destroyed most of the abbey, but the church survived.

During the war, the cafés here were some of the few places with heating. Writers spent all day nursing a single coffee, creating the café culture that defines French intellectual life.

Insider Tip

While Les Deux Magots is famous and expensive, the church interior is often overlooked and free. The painted pillars are stunningly colorful, unlike most grey Gothic churches. Step inside for five minutes of cool, quiet reflection.

Lunch Option

Le Comptoir du Relais at 9 Carrefour de l'Odéon is a bistronomy icon by Chef Yves Camdeborde. No reservations for lunch, arrive by 11:45 AM. Order the award-winning oeufs mayonnaise and braised beef cheek.

6

Pont des Arts

15 minutes Pont des Arts, 75006 Paris

A pedestrian-only wooden bridge offering the single most romantic view in Paris. Île de la Cité stretches to your left, the Louvre rises to your right. Built by Napoleon in 1804 as the first metal bridge in Paris, it was revolutionary in its time.

The love locks that once weighed down this bridge have been removed to save its structure. The transparent railings have restored the view that artists have painted for two centuries. Monet, Renoir, and countless others captured this exact scene.

Look downstream to see the tip of the Île de la Cité. It looks like the prow of a ship, which explains the Paris motto: Fluctuat nec mergitur. Tossed but not sunk.

Photo Op

Sunset apéro on Pont des Arts is a Parisian tradition. Locals bring wine and cheese. If you are here at golden hour, linger. The light on the Louvre facade is unforgettable.

Cour Carrée du Louvre

20 minutes Cour Carrée, Musée du Louvre, 75001 Paris

Before it was a museum, the Louvre was the royal palace. This courtyard shows the transition from medieval fortress to Renaissance palace. You can still see the outline of the original medieval dungeon keep paved into the ground in the center.

Look for the monograms on the building facades. H for Henry, K for Katherine. Each side of the courtyard was built by a different king, in a different era, in a different style. The result is an architectural history lesson written in stone.

This square is often empty and silent, a stark contrast to the chaotic Pyramid entrance just a few hundred meters away. Tourists rush to the museum. The wise linger here.

Insider Tip

At night, the courtyard is dramatically lit. If you return after dark, you will see the Louvre as the kings saw it: mysterious, imposing, eternal.

8

Jardin du Palais Royal

30 minutes 2 Galerie de Montpensier, 75001 Paris

A stunning, enclosed garden surrounded by arcades. It feels exclusive and hidden, despite being next to the Louvre. Built for Cardinal Richelieu, it later became a hub for revolutionaries, gamblers, and prostitutes in the 18th century before becoming the quiet sanctuary it is today.

The Colonnes de Buren, the black and white striped columns in the inner courtyard, are a controversial modern art installation from 1986. Parisians hated them at first. Now they are beloved, used as seats, photo backdrops, and playgrounds.

Read the small brass plaques on the benches. Many are touching love dedications from Parisians. This is where the city keeps its secrets and its hearts.

Insider Tip

The arcades contain some of Paris's most interesting boutiques and one of its oldest restaurants, Le Grand Véfour, where Napoleon and Josephine once dined. Window shopping costs nothing.

End of Tour Option

Juveniles Wine Bar at 47 Rue de Richelieu is a cult favorite for industry insiders. Great wines and unpretentious, high-quality food. Try the country terrine or the daily market special. Perfect reward after five hours of walking.

The End of Your Walk

You have walked from the medieval heart of Paris to its royal gardens, from the quiet island where time stopped to the grand courtyards where history was made. You crossed the Seine twice, passed through the Latin Quarter where students once spoke Latin, and ended in a cardinal's secret garden. This is Paris: layers upon layers of history, each step revealing another chapter. The metro awaits at Palais Royal-Musée du Louvre, but perhaps you want one more glass of wine first.
